Unveiling the Secrets Behind YouTube Categories

How does content diversity on YouTube influence audience engagement and channel growth?

Introduction

With millions of videos and hundreds of thousands of channels, YouTube is a content universe. We explored whether channels benefit from focusing on a single category or covering many topics. Using the large-scale YouNiverse dataset, we reveal how content variety correlates with subscriber counts, engagement, and long-term growth.

The Data

The YouNiverse dataset contains over 136,000 English-language YouTube channels and more than 72 million videos. We focused on channels with more than 10k subscribers and at least 10 videos. This allowed us to capture established creators and avoid noise from very small channels.

Research Questions

  1. Do YouTube channels with diverse content attract larger audiences and higher growth rates than those focused on a single topic?
  2. How does content diversity affect viewer engagement and loyalty?
  3. Is there a tipping point in content variety where audience fragmentation outweighs engagement benefits?
  4. Are there any relationships between categories?
  5. Which combination of categories is most effective for audience growth?
  6. Is it possible to predict the categorical cluster of a channel giving timeseries data?

I. Global Analysis of Categories

We began by examining the distribution of channel categories. Four categories—Music, Entertainment, Gaming, and People & Blogs—dominate both the number of channels and total subscribers.

These dominant categories account for over 60% of channels and subscribers, shaping much of YouTube’s content landscape.

II. Content Ecosystems: Co-occurrence of Categories

Channels rarely produce content exclusively in one category. We mapped out category co-occurrences to uncover content 'ecosystems'.

Entertainment and People & Blogs act as hubs, connecting various categories. A network of frequently co-occurring categories—Gaming, Music, Comedy, Film & Animation—emerges, suggesting these genres often blend together.

III. A Channel Diversity Metric

To quantify how varied a channel’s content is, we developed a metric that accounts for both the spread of categories and how related they are. Channels that cover many unrelated categories score higher on diversity than those focused on a single theme or closely related topics.

We found that most channels lean towards lower diversity. A Beta distribution fits the diversity scores well, indicating that extreme variety is rare.

IV. Finding the Optimal Diversity Range

Does more diversity always mean better performance? We split channels into low, moderate, and high diversity groups and examined their subscriber counts.

Surprisingly, channels in the moderate diversity range attracted more subscribers, suggesting a sweet spot where creators benefit from some variety without confusing their audience.

V. Evolution of Channels Over Time

We compared how channels in entertainment-oriented categories (Gaming, Music, Film & Animation) differ from those in educational categories (Education, How-to, Science & Tech) over their first year.

Entertainment channels experience sharp spikes in views tied to viral moments, followed by declines. Educational channels grow more steadily, showing the lasting value of evergreen content.

Subscriber trends mirror views: Entertainment sees intense but short-lived surges, while educational channels accumulate subscribers gradually and more consistently over time.

Conclusion

This analysis highlights that while YouTube’s most popular categories dominate overall numbers, sustainable growth and engagement often come from balanced content strategies. Moderately diverse channels fare better in attracting loyal subscribers, and content with lasting relevance—like educational videos—builds stable growth patterns over time.

These insights can guide creators in optimizing their content strategies: balancing variety with a recognizable identity may be the key to both attracting and retaining an engaged audience.
